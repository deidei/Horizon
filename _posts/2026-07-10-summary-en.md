## [GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra Proves Cycle Double Cover Conjecture](https://cdn.openai.com/pdf/04d1d1e4-bc75-476a-97cf-49055cd98d31/cdc_proof.pdf) ⭐️ 10.0/10

On July 10, 2026, OpenAI announced that its GPT-5.6 Sol Ultra model generated a proof of the Cycle Double Cover Conjecture, a longstanding open problem in graph theory. The proof and the prompt used are publicly released as PDFs. This marks a major milestone in AI-driven mathematical discovery, demonstrating that large language models can solve deep open problems in mathematics. It could transform how mathematicians approach conjectures and accelerate proof generation. The proof is concise, suggesting a clever trick previously missed by experts. The model was given a prompt that likely included background and instructions, and it produced the proof in about one hour.

hackernews · scrlk · Jul 10, 18:29 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48863490)

**Background**: The Cycle Double Cover Conjecture, posed by W. T. Tutte and others, asks whether every bridgeless undirected graph has a collection of cycles covering each edge exactly twice. It is a central problem in graph theory with connections to graph embeddings. Previously, only partial results existed, and the conjecture remained open for decades.

## [Long March 10B Achieves First Net-Based Sea Recovery](https://weibo.com/7340734455/R814of1Ki) ⭐️ 9.0/10

On July 10, 2026, the Long March 10B rocket launched from Hainan Commercial Spaceport and successfully recovered its first stage via a net-based system on a sea platform, marking China's first controlled first-stage recovery and the world's first net-based rocket recovery. This breakthrough significantly reduces launch costs and advances reusable rocket technology in China, demonstrating an independent recovery method that challenges existing approaches like SpaceX's landing legs. The net-based system uses a ship with a well-shaped arresting cable (阻拦索) to hook the rocket's attachment point, eliminating the need for landing legs. Long March 10B thus becomes China's first recoverable reusable rocket.

**Background**: Rocket recovery is essential for reusability, reducing cost per launch. Existing methods, such as SpaceX's vertical landing with legs, require precise engine burns and additional mass. Net capture offers an alternative that catches the rocket using cables, potentially reducing weight and complexity. China had not previously recovered a rocket first stage at sea.

This regulation strengthens consumer rights by making it as easy to cancel a subscription as to sign up, potentially forcing companies to improve transparency and reducing unwanted charges for millions of residents. ## [Successful Companies Develop Institutional Blindness](https://ianreppel.org/how-successful-companies-go-blind/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

Ian Reppel's article 'Successful Companies Go Blind' argues that as companies grow successful, bureaucratic inertia, risk aversion, and siloed management create institutional blindness, suppressing innovation. This pattern explains why many industry leaders fail to adapt to disruption, affecting employees, investors, and the broader economy. The article highlights that institutional blindness is not due to incompetence but systemic issues, and that even talented individuals become ineffective within thick bureaucracies.

hackernews · speckx · Jul 10, 13:31 · [Discussion](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48859678)

**Background**: Institutional blindness refers to organizations' tendency to overlook obvious problems due to ingrained processes and culture. Bureaucratic inertia is the tendency of bureaucracies to perpetuate established procedures, while siloed management occurs when departments operate in isolation. These factors together can stifle innovation and lead to organizational decline.

## [Why no submission limits per author in ML?](https://www.reddit.com/r/MachineLearning/comments/1usq43t/why_doesnt_the_ml_research_community_limit_the/) ⭐️ 8.0/10

A Reddit user points out that unlike fields like security (CCS) and computer architecture (DAC), the ML research community does not cap the number of submissions per author, exacerbating reviewer overload and quality issues seen in venues like ACL Rolling Review (ARR). Implementing per-author submission caps could reduce the review burden, improve the quality of reviews, and encourage deeper, more impactful work rather than mass submissions, potentially shifting ML research culture towards sustainability. The poster notes that other fields have successfully used submission limits for years; examples include CCS (security) and DAC (computer architecture). The question highlights a cultural difference in ML, where unlimited submissions are the norm despite growing pressure on the review system.

reddit · r/MachineLearning · /u/alafaya101 · Jul 10, 14:59

**Background**: ML conferences like NeurIPS, ICML, and ACL have seen a surge in submissions, straining the peer review process. ACL Rolling Review (ARR) is a centralized review platform for NLP conferences that aims to streamline reviews but still faces bottlenecks. In contrast, some other CS communities cap submissions per author to distribute the workload more evenly.

## [EU Fines Meta $12 Billion Over Addictive Design](https://www.theverge.com/policy/963872/meta-eu-addictive-design-200b-fine-risk-digital-services-act-dsa) ⭐️ 8.0/10

The European Commission preliminarily found that Meta's Facebook and Instagram violate the Digital Services Act due to addictive design features like infinite scroll, autoplay, and personalized recommendations. Meta risks a fine of up to $12 billion, equivalent to 6% of its global annual revenue. This is one of the strongest regulatory actions under the EU's Digital Services Act, directly targeting design practices that harm user well-being. It sets a precedent for platform accountability and could force Meta and other tech giants to fundamentally redesign core features to reduce addictive potential. The EU criticized Meta's existing time-limiting tools as ineffective and demanded default disabling of addictive features, effective screen time limits, and weakening of engagement-driven recommendation algorithms. A final decision could require Meta to redesign its applications entirely.

telegram · zaihuapd · Jul 10, 14:47

**Background**: The Digital Services Act (DSA) is an EU regulation effective since 2022 that imposes strict obligations on digital platforms, especially very large online platforms with over 45 million EU users. Addictive design patterns exploit psychological vulnerabilities to drive compulsive usage, causing users to spend excessive time or money. The DSA aims to protect users from such harmful practices and holds platforms accountable for their design choices.
